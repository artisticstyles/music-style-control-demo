## Paper Abstract

Text-conditioned music generation models can capture broad attributes such as instrumentation or mood, but control over finer stylistic properties remains limited. Prior approaches to personalization often involve retraining or specialized conditioning, which is costly and difficult to reproduce. We investigate whether lightweight, human-readable prompt modifiers, obtained automatically from a large language model, can shift the distribution of generated outputs toward that of a target artist without modifying the generator. Using MusicGen-small, we evaluate two contrasting cases: Billie Eilish, a vocal-heavy pop artist, and Ludovico Einaudi, an instrumental composer. Each artist is represented by fifteen reference excerpts, and outputs are generated from baseline prompts and from five sets of descriptor-augmented prompts under matched seeds. Evaluation in VGGish embedding space includes Frechet Audio Distance (FAD_vgg), centroid similarity, precision, recall, and diversity, with paired Wilcoxon tests across descriptor sets. Results show consistent improvements for both artists: Billie exhibits stronger gains in distributional alignment (-11% FAD_vgg, +99% recall), while Einaudi shows larger improvements in clip-level similarity and coverage (+2.5% cosine, +220% precision). A cross-artist control confirms specificity, with same-artist descriptors improving alignment and cross-artist transfers degrading it. These findings establish a reproducible protocol for evaluating prompt-level controllability in music generation, demonstrate that LLM-proposed descriptors are effective across both vocal and instrumental domains, and highlight domain-specific differences in how controllability is expressed.
